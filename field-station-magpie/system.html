The future of MAGPIE is produced by systems already in motion.

This section traces the past and present trajectories that appear to converge in the world represented by the archive: automation, corporate sovereignty, climate disruption, grid dependence, surveillance, logistics, and the erosion of individualized embodied life.

01 / Documented conditions

What We Know

  • The recovered material depicts a world shaped by extensive automation.
  • Corporate and governmental functions appear increasingly difficult to separate.
  • CorpSovs control infrastructure, access, mobility, and information across large territories.
  • Climate pressures shape settlement, resource distribution, and political control.
  • Daily life is deeply mediated by the grid.
02 / Systemic interpretation

What We Believe

  • Automation gradually displaced human labor, judgment, and local autonomy.
  • Corporate infrastructure became essential public infrastructure, then governing power.
  • Climate adaptation intensified inequality and territorial fragmentation.
  • Life within the grid reduced opportunities for private, embodied, and locally specific experience.
  • Resistance persisted through field stations, informal networks, and physical artifacts.
03 / Structural unknowns

What We Don’t Know

  • When corporate-state convergence became irreversible.
  • Whether CorpSovs replaced governments or absorbed them.
  • Who retained meaningful life outside the grid.
  • Whether the system collapsed, stabilized, or fragmented before 2147.
  • How much of the archive reflects ordinary life versus exceptional survival conditions.
04 / Research trajectory

What’s Next

  • Connect archived institutions to present-day historical precedents.
  • Map CorpSovs, field stations, and infrastructure across time.
  • Trace how automation altered labor, governance, and personal agency.
  • Compare climate records with migration and territorial evidence.
  • Identify where embodied life persisted beyond the grid.
